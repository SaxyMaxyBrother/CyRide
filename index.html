Why Weekend CyRide Service Needs to Improve

1. Student life is weekend-heavy

Classes may taper off Friday afternoon, but student life at Iowa State becomes busiest on Friday nights and weekends — from late-night studying and group projects to jobs, games, residence hall events, and social life.

Many campus and community events — athletic games, club meetings, concerts, and trips to essential places like grocery stores — happen on Saturdays and Sundays. When buses are less frequent or skip certain corridors, students without cars face long waits or expensive rideshares.

2. Weekend routes & frequency are limited

CyRide’s school-year schedule includes weekday, Saturday, Sunday, and Moonlight Express services. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} But the list of weekend routes is noticeably shorter.

On Saturdays, 10 routes operate (#1 Red East/West, #2 Green East/West, #3 Blue, #5 Yellow, #6 Brown North/South, #9 Plum, #11 Cherry). On Sundays, service drops to 9 routes, with #5 Yellow not running. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} Other routes like #7 Purple, #12 Lilac, and #21 Cardinal are more limited or do not run under some reduced schedules. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Fewer routes and longer headways mean some apartments, jobs, and stores become much harder to reach on weekends.

3. Safety depends on more than Moonlight Express

Moonlight Express offers free late-night rides within Ames on Friday and Saturday nights from about 10:30 p.m. to 2:30 a.m. when ISU is in session. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

This is an incredible service for late-night safety — especially after social events and downtown activities. But outside that time window, students traveling on weekend mornings, afternoons, or early evenings still face long waits or gaps in coverage. Better daytime and evening service complements Moonlight Express and keeps more students off the road.

4. Ridership is high enough to justify better weekends

CyRide’s fixed-route ridership was over 4.7 million trips in 2024, up nearly 14% from 2023 as riders return after the pandemic. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

That level of demand suggests that improving weekend access isn’t a luxury — it’s a response to how often students, staff, and community members already rely on the bus. With so many riders connected to ISU, making weekend service stronger directly supports student success and equity.

Student Weekend Experiences

These are example scenarios showing how weekend transit gaps affect students. (You can replace these with real quotes from classmates if you gather them.)

Group Projects Off-Campus

A group project meets at a teammate’s apartment off Lincoln Way on Sunday afternoon. With fewer routes and less frequent service, some group members have to leave over an hour early or rely on friends with cars.

Weekend Jobs & Shifts

Many students work retail, food service, or campus jobs that include weekend shifts. When buses run less often, a missed connection means being late to work or needing an expensive rideshare.

Groceries & Basic Needs

Students without cars depend on CyRide to buy groceries, pick up prescriptions, and run errands. Weekend gaps can mean putting off basic needs or carrying heavy bags long distances from the nearest stop that does have service.

Late-Night Safety

Moonlight Express is a critical safety service, but its hours (about 10:30 p.m.–2:30 a.m.) don’t help when someone is stuck at a friend’s place at 3 a.m. or needs to get home from a late study session that runs past normal routes but before Moonlight starts. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}
