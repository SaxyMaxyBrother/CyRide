Better CyRide on Weekends

Making weekend transit match student life

Overview Why Weekends Evidence Student Stories Vote

CyRide runs seven days a week. Weekends shouldn’t feel like an afterthought.

On weekdays, buses feel constant and reliable. On weekends, routes shrink, waits stretch past 30–40 minutes, and students without cars are the first ones stranded. This site argues for stronger weekend service so student life, jobs, and safety don’t pause after Friday night.

What’s Broken on Weekends?

When buses thin out on Saturdays and Sundays, it quietly decides who gets to participate fully in campus life and who has to stay home. These four issues show why weekend service needs an update.

1. Long waits

A 10-minute ride shouldn’t require a 45-minute wait.

On many corridors, weekday buses come often enough that missing one isn’t a crisis. On weekends, the same stops can go quiet for half an hour or longer. For students who work, study, or run errands without a car, one missed bus can mean being late to a shift or skipping plans entirely.

2. Fewer routes

Some apartments might as well disappear on Sundays.

Weekend schedules cut back on which routes run. That means some apartment complexes, jobs, and stores lose direct service or only get a bus every once in a while. The parts of Ames where students already rely on transit the most become harder to reach right when they finally have time to use them.

3. Safety gaps

“Just Uber home” isn’t a safety plan.

Moonlight Express is a huge step toward safer late-night travel, but it doesn’t cover every time students are out. Study groups, campus jobs, and social events often end right as regular buses stop but before Moonlight starts. A stronger weekend schedule would support safety before things become an emergency.

4. Equity & access

Weekends shouldn’t reward “who owns a car.”

Students who don’t have cars, don’t want to drive late, or share a vehicle with family are hit hardest by thin weekend service. When buses slow down, participation in jobs, clubs, and community events becomes a privilege tied to money and transportation, not effort or interest.

Student Weekend Experiences

These scenarios are based on real patterns at Iowa State. They show how weekend transit gaps shape what students can actually do with their “free time.”

Saturday grocery run

Maya plans to do a quick grocery run between noon and her 2 p.m. study group. The bus to the store comes only once every 40 minutes on Saturdays. To avoid missing her ride back, she rushes through shopping and still ends up late to her group project.

Off-campus weekend shift

Jorge works a Saturday evening shift at a restaurant on South Duff. On weekdays he can time a bus easily. On weekends, the gap between buses is so long that he either arrives 30 minutes early and waits in the back, or gambles on being late and stressing the whole way there.

Sunday night group work

A group for an engineering project meets at a teammate’s off-campus apartment Sunday night. Two members without cars leave the meeting early to catch the last bus. They miss part of the planning conversation and end up doing extra work later just to keep up.

Choosing between safety and cost

After a residence hall event, Taylor and their friends realize the bus they counted on doesn’t run this late on weekends. They split an expensive rideshare home because walking across town at midnight doesn’t feel safe. The next time, they decide not to go at all.
