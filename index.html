Better CyRide on Weekends

Making weekend transit match student life

Overview Why Weekends Matter Evidence Student Voices Vote

CyRide runs 7 days a week — but weekends lag behind.

CyRide is the city bus system for Ames, operated in partnership with Iowa State University and ISU Student Government. It runs 13 fixed routes plus Dial-A-Ride and the late-night Moonlight Express. On paper, it’s a seven-day system — but in practice, weekend service is significantly more limited in both routes and frequency.

Why Weekend CyRide Service Needs to Improve

1. Student life is weekend-heavy

Classes may taper off Friday afternoon, but student life at Iowa State becomes busiest on Friday nights and weekends — from games and residence hall events to late-night studying, jobs, and social life.

Many campus and community activities — athletic events, club meetings, concerts, and trips to essential places like grocery stores — happen on Saturdays and Sundays. When buses are less frequent or skip certain corridors, students without cars face long waits, limited choices, or expensive rideshares.

2. Weekend routes & frequency are limited

During the school year, CyRide’s full network includes 13 fixed routes, but only a subset of these run on weekends. Weekend schedules group service into fewer routes with longer gaps between buses.

On typical school-year Saturdays, service focuses on routes like #1 Red (East & West), #2 Green (East & West), #3 Blue, #5 Yellow, #6 Brown (North & South), #9 Plum, and #11 Cherry. On Sundays, service is even narrower — for example, Sunday schedules often look similar but without #5 Yellow. That means fewer options for reaching off-campus apartments, jobs, and stores on weekends.

Fewer routes and longer headways mean some parts of Ames are effectively “cut off” compared to the robust weekday network.

3. Safety depends on more than Moonlight Express

Moonlight Express is CyRide’s free late-night service that runs Friday and Saturday nights from about 10:30 p.m. to 2:30 a.m. when ISU is in full session. It’s designed to give students a safe ride home.

Moonlight Express is an amazing safety resource — especially after downtown or Campustown events. But outside that time window, students traveling on weekend mornings, afternoons, or early evenings still face gaps in service. Better daytime and evening weekend service would complement Moonlight Express and keep more students out of unsafe situations.

4. High ridership justifies better weekends

CyRide provided roughly 4.7 million rides in FY 2024, making it one of the most heavily used transit systems in Iowa when measured per resident in its service area.

As ridership rebounds after the pandemic, demand is again concentrated around Iowa State. Students, staff, and community members increasingly rely on CyRide not just for commuting, but for everything from work to healthcare to groceries. Improving weekend service is not a luxury — it matches how people actually move around Ames.

Student Weekend Experiences

These are example scenarios that illustrate how weekend transit gaps affect students. You can replace them with real quotes from ISU students if you collect them for this project.

Group Projects Off-Campus

A group project meets at a teammate’s apartment off Lincoln Way on Sunday afternoon. With fewer routes and less frequent service, some group members have to leave over an hour early or depend on friends with cars just to make it on time.

Weekend Jobs & Shifts

Many students work retail, food-service, or campus jobs that include weekend shifts. When buses run less often, a missed connection means being late to work, and rideshares can eat a big chunk of a student paycheck.

Groceries & Basic Needs

Students without cars use CyRide to access grocery stores, pharmacies, and other essentials. Weekend gaps can force them to delay basic needs or carry heavy bags long distances from the nearest stop that actually has weekend service.

Late-Night Safety

Moonlight Express helps students get home safely late at night on Fridays and Saturdays, but its limited hours still leave some travel times uncovered. For students staying late at friends’ apartments, at the library, or at events off-campus, more frequent weekend routes and slightly extended hours could make a big difference.
